# PART 2: ANATOMY â€” THE FOUNDATION

### Q: What is the A-Ring?
**A:** Muscular ring ABOVE the vestibule. It is DYNAMIC (moves on swallow).

### Q: What is the B-Ring (Schatzki Ring)?
**A:** MUCOSAL ring at the GE junction. It is STATIC (does NOT move).

> ðŸ§  **MNEMONIC:** "**A** = **A**ctive (dynamic). **B** = **B**locked in place (static)."

### Q: What is the Z-Line?
**A:** The squamocolumnar junction. Rarely seen on barium, standard on endoscopy.

**A:** Level C5-C6. The TRUE upper esophageal sphincter. Creates a smooth POSTERIOR indentation.

> ðŸ§  **MNEMONIC:** "**C**ricopharyngeus at **C**5-**C**6."

# PART 3: ACHALASIA

### Q: What is the pathophysiology of achalasia?
**A:** Failure of LES to relax due to destruction of myenteric ganglia (Auerbach plexus).

### Q: What is the classic barium sign of achalasia?
**A:** **"Bird's Beak"** â€” smooth, tapered distal narrowing.

### Q: What are the two causes of achalasia?
**A:** Idiopathic (most common) or Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi â€” think South America).

### Q: What are the TWO complications of achalasia?
**A:** 1) Candida esophagitis (from stasis) and 2) Squamous Cell Carcinoma (20-year lag).

> âš¡ **HIGH YIELD:** Achalasia â†’ SCC (not adeno). 20-year lag. This is a classic FMH2 trap.

### Q: What does achalasia look like on barium?
**A:** Massively dilated esophagus, debris ("cheerios and mashed potatoes"), ABSENT primary peristalsis, bird's beak distally.

### Q: What does achalasia look like on CT?
**A:** Massively dilated, fluid/debris-filled esophagus. No air in stomach (because LES won't open).

### Q: What is pseudoachalasia?
**A:** Cancer at the GEJ mimicking achalasia. Key difference: does NOT relax with standing. Fixed obstruction.

> ðŸ”´ **TRAP:** True achalasia CAN show transient LES relaxation on standing. Pseudoachalasia CANNOT. If it's fixed â†’ think cancer.

### Q: What is "Vigorous Achalasia"?
**A:** Early form with repetitive simultaneous NON-propulsive contractions. The esophagus isn't dilated yet.

### Q: What are the 3 treatments for achalasia?
**A:** Pneumatic dilation, Heller myotomy, POEM (peroral endoscopic myotomy).

> ðŸ§  **MNEMONIC:** "**P**OP the bird's beak: **P**neumatic, **O**perative (Heller), **P**OEM."

# PART 4: SCLERODERMA

### Q: What percentage of scleroderma patients have esophageal involvement?
**A:** **80%** â€” the esophagus is the most commonly affected GI organ.

### Q: What is the pathophysiology of scleroderma esophagus?
**A:** Smooth muscle atrophy and fibrosis of the DISTAL 2/3.

> âš¡ **HIGH YIELD:** Why distal 2/3? Because the UPPER 1/3 is striated muscle (unaffected). Scleroderma attacks SMOOTH muscle only.

### Q: What is the classic sign of scleroderma esophagus?
**A:** **"Patulous" LES** â€” permanently open, incompetent sphincter.

### Q: What lung pattern is associated with scleroderma?
**A:** **NSIP** â€” basal-predominant lung fibrosis.

### Q: What esophageal complication does scleroderma cause?
**A:** Free reflux â†’ Barrett's esophagus (because the LES is wide open).

### Q: Scleroderma vs Achalasia â€” how do you tell them apart?
**A:**
| | Scleroderma | Achalasia |
|---|---|---|
| **LES** | OPEN (patulous) | CLOSED (bird's beak) |
| **Reflux** | YES (free) | NO |
| **Stomach air** | YES | NO |

> ðŸ§  **MNEMONIC:** "**S**cleroderma = **S**phincter is **S**lack. **A**chalasia = **A**ll shut."

### Q: Can esophageal dilation appear before skin changes in scleroderma?
**A:** YES â€” the esophagus can be the first clue.

# PART 5: REFLUX & BARRETT'S

### Q: What are the risk factors for reflux esophagitis?
**A:** Chronic GERD, Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (excess acid).

### Q: What does reflux look like on barium?
**A:** Fold thickening, mucosal granularity, DISTAL strictures.

### Q: What is the classic barium sign of Barrett's?
**A:** **Reticular mucosal pattern** (lace-like).

### Q: Where are Barrett's strictures located?
**A:** **MID-esophageal** (HIGH stricture), associated with a hiatal hernia.

> âš¡ **HIGH YIELD:** Why mid-esophageal? Barrett's metaplastic tissue is ACID-RESISTANT. The acid damages the normal squamous tissue ABOVE Barrett's â†’ stricture forms at the junction.

### Q: Barrett's is a precursor to which cancer?
**A:** **Adenocarcinoma** (NOT squamous cell).

> ðŸ§  **MEMORY HOOK:** "**B**arrett's â†’ **B**ottom of esophagus â†’ **A**denocarcinoma. **A**chalasia â†’ **A**nywhere â†’ **S**quamous."

### Q: What can reflux esophagitis cause structurally?
**A:** Esophageal shortening â†’ hiatal hernia formation.

# PART 6: INFECTIOUS ESOPHAGITIS

### Q: What is the classic appearance of Candida esophagitis?
**A:** **"Shaggy" contour** with discrete plaque-like lesions.

### Q: In what clinical context does Candida esophagitis occur?
**A:** HIV, transplant recipients, or ACHALASIA (stasis).

**A:** **Glycogenic acanthosis** â€” small mucosal nodules in asymptomatic elderly patients. NOT infectious.

# PART 7: DIVERTICULA

### Q: Where is Zenker's diverticulum and which direction does it go?
**A:** Hypopharynx (ABOVE cricopharyngeus). Goes POSTERIORLY. Through Killian's dehiscence.

### Q: Where is Killian-Jamieson diverticulum?

> ðŸ”´ **TRAP:** Zenker = ABOVE CP, posterior. Killian-Jamieson = BELOW CP, anterolateral. Examiners LOVE this distinction.

### Q: What is a traction diverticulum?

**A:** Distal esophagus (above diaphragm). Associated with motor disorders (achalasia, DES).

# PART 8: ESOPHAGEAL CANCER

### Q: Where does Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC) occur?
**A:** MID to UPPER esophagus.

### Q: What are the risk factors for SCC?
**A:** Smoking, Alcohol, Lye (caustic) ingestion, Achalasia (20-year lag).
